COMPREHENSIVE MULTI-PART PHILOSOPHY AND ETHICS EXAMINATION

This assignment covers four distinct topic areas. You must address EVERY part fully and thoroughly. Total expected length: 1800–2200 words. Do not skip any section.

PART ONE: KANT'S DEONTOLOGICAL ETHICS (400–500 words)

Explain Immanuel Kant's deontological ethical theory in detail. Your response must address all of the following:

1. The concept of the categorical imperative in its three main formulations: (a) the Formula of Universal Law, (b) the Formula of Humanity, and (c) the Formula of the Kingdom of Ends.
2. The distinction Kant draws between hypothetical imperatives and categorical imperatives, and why only the latter can ground genuine moral obligation.
3. The concept of the "good will" and why Kant considers it the only thing good without qualification.
4. At least two concrete ethical examples that illustrate how the categorical imperative applies in practice (e.g., lying, promise-keeping, suicide).
5. The concept of moral autonomy and how it distinguishes rational agents from mere things.

PART TWO: UTILITARIAN ETHICS AND ITS TENSIONS WITH KANTIANISM (400–500 words)

Explain utilitarianism in its classical Benthamite and Millian forms. Then analyze the fundamental conflicts between utilitarian and Kantian ethics. Your response must address:

1. The principle of utility (greatest happiness principle) and how it functions as a decision procedure.
2. The distinction between act-utilitarianism and rule-utilitarianism, with an example of a case where they diverge.
3. Mill's qualitative distinction between higher and lower pleasures and whether this modification weakens or strengthens classical utilitarianism.
4. A specific moral dilemma (e.g., the trolley problem, torture of one to save many) where Kantian and utilitarian verdicts conflict, with a rigorous analysis of both sides.
5. Which framework you find more defensible, with three explicit reasons, while acknowledging the strongest objection to your position.

PART THREE: APPLIED ETHICS — ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AND MORAL STATUS (400–500 words)

Examine the ethical implications of advanced artificial intelligence systems. Apply both Kantian and utilitarian frameworks from Parts One and Two. Address:

1. Whether a sufficiently advanced AI system could possess moral status, and what criteria (sentience, rationality, autonomy) would be required under each ethical framework.
2. The ethical obligations of AI developers and corporations under the categorical imperative — specifically, what universal maxims should govern AI deployment.
3. The utilitarian calculus of AI risk: how to weigh the aggregate benefits of AI automation (efficiency, medical advances, scientific discovery) against risks of job displacement, surveillance, and existential risk.
4. The concept of "epistemic autonomy" — whether AI-generated content undermines human rational agency — and how Kant's Formula of Humanity applies to systems that influence belief formation at scale.
5. A concrete policy recommendation (minimum 2 sentences) grounded in your ethical analysis.

PART FOUR: METAETHICS — THE GROUNDING OF MORAL FACTS (400–500 words)

Step back from first-order ethical questions and examine the metaethical foundations of the theories discussed above. Address:

1. The distinction between moral realism, anti-realism, and constructivism. Which metaethical position does Kantian ethics most naturally align with, and why?
2. The is-ought problem (Hume's guillotine): how utilitarians and Kantians each attempt to derive prescriptive moral claims from descriptive facts about the world, and whether either attempt succeeds.
3. The relationship between moral epistemology and the two first-order theories: how do we come to know moral truths under each framework?
4. The challenge of moral relativism: if moral facts are mind-dependent or culturally constructed, what follows for the universal pretensions of both Kantian and utilitarian ethics?
5. Your own metaethical verdict: which framework has the more defensible metaethical foundation, with explicit argumentation.
